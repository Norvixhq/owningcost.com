AI roadmap

AI as a layer over the calculators — not a replacement for the math.

OwningCost's AI direction is deliberate. Every AI tool sits on top of a deterministic calculator that does the math. The AI handles the language layer — explaining results, framing trade-offs, helping you reason through scenarios. The numbers stay verifiable. This page documents what we're building, what we're building first, and what we will not promise.

In development Calculator-first Math is auditable
What AI means here

What "AI tools" means at OwningCost.

It does not mean a chat interface that produces housing advice from training data. That's the most common pattern in 2026 and the worst-suited to financial decisions. We are deliberately not building it.

What it means here is more specific and, we think, more useful: an AI layer that converts a person's situation in natural language into calculator inputs, runs the calculator deterministically, and then explains the resulting numbers in plain language. The math is always the calculator's. The AI's job is translation in both directions — from English into a calculator's input fields, and from the calculator's output back into language that contextualizes what the numbers mean for this specific person.

Every AI answer on the platform is auditable. Click through, see the inputs the AI set, change anything, rerun. The numbers don't change because the AI rephrased the question. If the AI's translation got something wrong, the inputs are visible and editable. That accountability loop is non-negotiable for us, and it's why we frame our AI work as a layer over calculators rather than a chat-shaped product.

Why we're not building generic chat for housing

Housing-cost decisions involve real money, real uncertainty, and a regulatory environment that punishes confidently-wrong answers. A model that hallucinates a property tax rate, an FHA MIP duration, or a PMI removal rule produces output that looks like advice and is sometimes wrong in ways the user can't detect. Even when the model is right on average, the failure mode of being confidently wrong on a specific question is asymmetric — a $400 monthly cost error compounded over a 30-year hold is a six-figure mistake.

The fix isn't more disclaimers. The fix is to put the math somewhere the model isn't doing it. Our calculators are deterministic, formulas are documented in the methodology page, and the AI's job is to be useful around them — not to replace them.

First tool

Explain My Output — the first AI tool we're shipping.

A small chatbot embedded directly on each calculator page that contextualizes your specific result in plain language.

Concretely: you run the True Monthly Cost calculator on a $425,000 home in 75034. It returns $3,533/month. The "Explain My Output" panel below the result tells you why — which line items account for the gap between the headline mortgage payment and the true monthly cost, which inputs are most sensitive (a 0.5% change in your tax rate changes the answer by how much?), and which assumptions are likely to be wrong for your specific situation. The numbers in the explanation come from the calculator. The AI is just narrating them in a way that's specific to you instead of generic.

Why this first, before anything else:

  • It doesn't require URL parsing, document understanding, or external data — just the calculator's output, which is already structured.
  • It runs against deterministic numbers, not free-text. The hallucination surface is small.
  • It falls back gracefully — when the model is uncertain about a specific framing, it can simply quote the calculator's own line items rather than synthesize.
  • It amplifies an existing surface (the calculator pages people already use) rather than creating a new product surface that has to earn its own traffic.
  • It's a plausible MVP scope — measurable in weeks of focused work, not quarters.

"AI explains your math" is true to the calculator-first brand. It's the smallest meaningful AI feature we can ship that improves the working surface for users who are already using OwningCost productively.

What's next, after Explain My Output ships

The roadmap, in order.

Each of these only gets serious build attention after the previous one ships and proves out. We aren't promising delivery dates because every tool below is contingent on what we learn from the one before it.

Phase 2 — Scenario Assistant

Describe a situation in plain English ("I'm in Frisco, looking at a $480K home, 10% down, my rate quote is 6.625%, planning to be there 7 years"); the assistant identifies which calculators apply, fills the inputs, runs them, and returns a structured summary. The behavioral test: a user gets a complete answer to "should I do this?" without having to know which calculator to start in. The mockchat on the Scenario Assistant page shows what this looks like at the output layer.

Phase 3 — Compare Two Loans

Paste two loan estimates; get a structured comparison over your real hold period rather than just headline rate. APR isn't enough — closing-cost differences, mortgage-insurance structures, and break-even time matter more for most borrowers. Foundation: the existing FHA vs. Conventional, VA vs. Conventional, and ARM vs. Fixed calculators. The AI layer extracts the numbers from pasted loan estimates and runs the comparisons.

Phase 4 — Should I Wait to Buy?

The most common decision question on the platform. Plain-English situation → runs Buy Now vs. Wait with sensible defaults for your context, surfaces the variables that actually move the answer (rent inflation, appreciation, rate trajectory), and explains which assumptions the verdict is most sensitive to. We'll likely build this third because it's the highest-volume question, but only after the explainer and assistant layers prove out.

Honest deferrals

Three tools currently sit on the public roadmap that we're not committing to at this time:

  • Listing Analyzer — accepting a Zillow or Redfin URL and extracting listing details. The major real-estate platforms actively block scraping with Cloudflare and bot detection, and the legal posture is unsettled. The user need is real but it's better solved by the existing Listing Reality Check tool, which asks for a few specific fields rather than a URL. We'd rather acknowledge the gap than ship a tool that breaks every time Zillow updates its anti-bot measures.
  • Compare Two Homes — runs into the same URL-parsing problem. The existing comparison calculators handle this when the user provides specifics manually.
  • ZIP Lookup as an AI product — this isn't really an AI tool; it's a data product. ZIP-level tax, insurance, and HOA data has licensing and accuracy requirements that the AI layer can't paper over. We will build ZIP intelligence, but as structured data with a clean lookup UI — not as an AI feature.

If any of these become buildable in a way that meets our quality bar — Listing Analyzer if real-estate platforms ever expose stable APIs, ZIP Lookup once a data partner makes it economically feasible — we'll revisit. We won't ship them just to fill out a roadmap grid.

How AI works with the rest of the platform

AI + calculators + ZIP intelligence.

The platform's architecture is calculator-first, ZIP-aware, and (eventually) AI-explained. Each layer is independent. The calculators work without the AI; the AI doesn't work without the calculators. ZIP intelligence is a data input that flows into every calculator regardless of whether the AI layer is present.

What this means in practice: every AI tool we ship will name the underlying calculator it sits on, link to it, and let the user open the calculator directly to verify, modify, or extend whatever the AI surfaced. There is no version of OwningCost in which the AI has access to information the user can't audit.

When ZIP intelligence ships, it will improve every calculator's defaults — taxes, insurance, maintenance reserves, and risk factors will become ZIP-specific instead of national-average. The AI tools, once they're live, will reference those defaults the same way they reference any other input. ZIP intelligence isn't an AI feature; it's a data feature that the AI uses, the same way every calculator does.

The boundary lines

What the AI will and won't do.

What the AI is for

  • Explaining a calculator's output in language tailored to your specific inputs
  • Surfacing which inputs are most sensitive to your specific decision
  • Translating a plain-English situation into the right calculator and the right inputs
  • Comparing scenarios you set up, with the math done by calculators
  • Identifying questions you didn't think to ask but should have

What the AI is not for

  • Quoting interest rates, tax rates, or insurance premiums from training data
  • Telling you what to do with your money
  • Replacing a conversation with a lender, real-estate agent, attorney, or CPA
  • Pretending to know your local market when ZIP-level data isn't yet available
  • Generating advice that sounds confident when the calculator hasn't actually run

How users should think about AI-generated guidance here

The same way you'd think about any other output on the site: useful when the inputs are accurate, only as good as the assumptions you set, and never a substitute for the professional advice that applies to your specific situation. The site's standing disclaimer applies to AI tools the same way it applies to calculators. The methodology page documents the underlying math; the AI doesn't change it.

If an AI explanation on this site ever quotes a number that doesn't trace back to a calculator's output, that's a bug. Tell us through the contact page and we'll fix it.

FAQ

Common AI questions.

When will Explain My Output actually ship?
We aren't committing to a date publicly. The reason is simple — every soft date we've watched other tools ship at has slipped, and we'd rather under-promise than over-promise. The work is in active development, the scope is small, and we'll announce a launch when it's ready for real users. Email or follow updates through the contact page if you want to be notified.
Will the AI ever give me actual financial advice?
No. OwningCost is not a financial advisor, and adding an AI layer doesn't change that. The AI explains math the calculator already did and surfaces decision factors. Whether you should actually do something — buy, refinance, sell — is a question for you and the relevant professional, not for the AI.
Why no chatbot? Every other site has one.
Because chatbots produce confident-looking text from training data, and confident-looking text about mortgages, taxes, and insurance is exactly the failure mode that hurts users in this space. The cost of being wrong is too asymmetric to ship a generic chat interface. The constraint of "AI only narrates calculator output" is the constraint that makes our approach safe.
Will the AI work on every calculator or only some?
The first version of Explain My Output will ship on the most-used calculators — True Monthly Cost, Affordability, Rent vs. Buy, and FHA vs. Conventional are the likely first set. Coverage will expand to the rest of the calculator catalog after the initial release proves out.
What happens to the existing AI pages while these are being built?
They stay live as roadmap documentation. Each AI page links prominently to the underlying calculator so users who arrive there can get to a working tool in one click. Once a tool ships, its page becomes the live tool. The pages that document deferred tools (Listing Analyzer, Compare Two Homes) explain why and point to the existing calculators that solve the underlying need.
Will using the AI cost anything?
No. OwningCost is free, has no signup, and runs no advertising. The AI tools will work the same way. If we ever need to introduce a paid tier to cover infrastructure costs at scale, the core calculators and the most-used AI tools will stay free; we'll be transparent about what's in the paid tier and why. We aren't there yet and may never be.
Does the AI use my inputs to train models?
No. The calculator math runs entirely in your browser today, and the AI tools when they ship will follow the same privacy posture — inputs are not stored, not trained on, not sold. The trust line above every calculator says exactly this; the AI tools will hold to the same standard.
